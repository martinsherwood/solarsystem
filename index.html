Our Solar System

Basic Facts:

A solar system refers to a star and all the objects that travel in orbit around it. Our solar system consists of the sun - our star - eight planets and their natural satellites (such as our moon); dwarf planets; asteroids and comets. Our solar system is located in an outward spiral of the Milky Way galaxy.

The eight planets are Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune.

How big is our solar system?

To think about the large distances, we use a cosmic ruler based on the astronomical unit (AU). One AU is the distance from Earth to the Sun, which is about 150 million kilometers or 93 million miles. Particles from the Sun can reach far beyond the planets, forming a giant bubble called the heliosphere. The enormous bubble of the heliosphere is created by the solar wind, a stream of charged gas blowing outward from the Sun. As the Sun orbits the center of the Milky Way, the bubble of the heliosphere moves also, creating a bow shock ahead of itself in interstellar space - like the bow of a ship in water - as it crashes into the interstellar gases. The region where the solar wind is abruptly slowed by pressure from gas between the stars is called the termination shock.

Help

  1. Click the Our Solar System title in the top, left to view some basic information and facts about the solar system.
  2. Select a planet in the selection bar across the bottom to view facts about that planet in hovering tool tips.
  3. Use the Velocity, Distance and Size buttons in the bottom, right corner to switch between the information being shown in the tool tips for the planets.
  4. Double click on on the planet name in the selection bar to view more information and details about that planet in an modal windows.
    • In those modal windows, you can click the speech icon in the bottom, left corner to have the information read to you by the browser.
  5. Hide all user interface elements by clicking the eye icon in the top, right corner of the screen.
